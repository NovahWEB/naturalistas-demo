Who we are

You can’t protect what you don’t know

Fundación Los Naturalistas was born in 2017 in Chiriquí, in the highlands of Boquete, founded by a group of Panamanian field biologists. One conviction brought us together: much of the country’s biodiversity is still undescribed, and it vanishes before we ever get to know it. We work to change that.

Our focus is amphibians and reptiles: the most threatened groups and, at the same time, the clearest indicators of an ecosystem’s health. We combine integrative taxonomy (morphology, bioacoustics and DNA barcoding) with field monitoring, community-based conservation and environmental education. Every new species we describe and every child who learns to look at a frog without fear is a step toward a Panama that knows and protects what’s its own.

“To describe a species is to give it a chance of being protected.”

What we do

Four fronts, one mission

01

Research

We describe Panama’s herpetofauna through integrative taxonomy: morphology, calls (bioacoustics) and DNA barcoding. That’s how we bring to light species hidden in plain sight. So far, more than 35 are new to science.

02

Conservation

We monitor critically endangered populations, such as the harlequin frogs (Atelopus), and design management plans together with the communities living in the country’s most remote corners, from the Darién to the highlands of Chiriquí.

03

Education

We train the next generation of naturalists with herpetology workshops, our “Science for Kids” program and teaching materials. When people get to know their biodiversity, they stop fearing it and start caring for it.

04

Citizen science

We publish free-to-download books and field guides and invite everyone to record what they see. Every outing into the forest can add a data point, and that data point counts for conservation.

Panama’s biodiversity · type specimen Flagship species · described with our team

Greta Thunberg’s rainfrog

Pristimantis gretathunbergae

Described in 2022 · Cerro Chucantí, Darién

In 2022 an international team including Los Naturalistas described this tiny rainfrog from the cloud forests of the Darién and named it after activist Greta Thunberg, because its habitat, like the climate, is in danger. Every new species is also a warning.

Foto: Mebert et al., ZooKeys 1081 (2022) · CC BY 4.0

Field catalogue

Panama is a bridge between two continents and two oceans, and that makes it one of the most biodiverse places on Earth. Species without a name still live in its cloud forests and rainforests. These are some of the ones we study, and several we helped describe for the first time.

Projects

Science that happens in the forest, not at a desk

P-01
Conservation Cerro Pirre, Darién Ongoing · UNACHI · Pijibasal

Saving the Pirre harlequin frog

We study the habitat and monitor Atelopus glyphus, a critically endangered harlequin frog, and build an education and conservation plan together with the Pijibasal community, in Cerro Pirre.

P-02
Research Darién National Park Since 2015 · GEMAS · Fondo Darién

Herpetofauna of the Darién

We assess the amphibians and reptiles of Darién National Park through transects across different forest types and elevations, to learn the true state of one of the most diverse and least explored rainforests in the Americas.

P-03
Education Panama · SENACYT · UNACHI FID 117-2017 · SENACYT + UNACHI

Amphibians at the Union of the Americas

We use frogs as a model to teach conservation: environmental education plans, monitoring protocols and resources like “Amphibian Detectives” to bring science into the classroom.

P-04
Research Across Panama Ongoing

Barcoding life

We combine DNA barcoding and integrative taxonomy to decode the true diversity of Panama’s herpetofauna, often hidden within species that look identical to the naked eye.

P-05
Education Chiriquí · communities Our own program

Science for Kids

Workshops where children handle up close the animals many people fear and learn to understand them. Demand says it all: nature, up close, stops being frightening.

Our team

Panamanian field biologists

AB

Dr. Abel Batista

President · Herpetologist

PhD biologist (Senckenberg / Goethe University, Frankfurt) with over 20 years of fieldwork across Panama, Costa Rica and Colombia. He has described more than 35 species new to science, including the frog named after Greta Thunberg. He researches bioacoustics, biogeography and taxonomy.

MP

Lic. Madian Pamela Miranda

Biologist · Conservation

UNACHI biologist specialized in amphibians and reptiles. Co-author of the book “Snakes of Panama” and part of the team that described Greta Thunberg’s rainfrog. At the foundation she leads the field expeditions and conservation work.

OG

Lic. Orlando Garcés

Biologist · Herpetology

Biologist focused on herpetology, conservation, taxonomy and nature photography. Member of the Mesoamerican and Caribbean Network for Amphibian and Reptile Conservation. He works with amphibians in captivity.

MQ

Michelle Quiroz

Researcher · Environmental education

She studies the Pirre harlequin frog (Atelopus glyphus) and leads environmental education with the Cerro Pirre communities, in the Darién.

MP

Marcos Ponce

Field biologist · Taxonomy

Field and taxonomy collaborator; he has taken part in expeditions and species descriptions alongside Abel Batista, including the monitoring of the Darién’s herpetofauna.
